Vector spaces

  • A real vector space is a non-empty set with:
    • vector addition (commutative, associative, has identity and inverse elements for all)
    • scalar multiplication (associative, has identity 1, distributes over both scalar and vector addition)
    • closure under the two
    • the scalar field R\mathbb{R}R﻿ (i.e. where the scalar multiplication gets the scalars from)
  • usu. just check for closure and the existence of the identity (esp. 0V0_V0V​﻿)
  • Other fields include C,Q,Zp,\mathbb{C},\mathbb Q,\mathbb{Z}_p,C,Q,Zp​,﻿ i.e. integers modulo some prime ppp﻿

Dimension and bases

  • Steinitz Exchange Lemma: in vector space VVV﻿, with X={vi}⊆VX=\{v_i\}\subseteq VX={vi​}⊆V﻿, if u∈⟨X⟩,u∉⟨X/{v}⟩u\in\langle X\rangle, u\notin\langle X/\{v\}\rangleu∈⟨X⟩,u∈/⟨X/{v}⟩﻿, then ⟨{u}∪X/{v}⟩=⟨X⟩\langle \{u\}\cup X/\{v\}\rangle=\langle X\rang⟨{u}∪X/{v}⟩=⟨X⟩﻿
    • that is, exchanging vvv﻿ for uuu﻿ does not affect the span, if the set “needs vvv﻿ to reach uuu﻿”
    • proof: just show that the two spans are subsets of each other by rearranging coefficients

      (with some care showing that the coefficient is non-0 when dividing)

  • given finite vector space VVV﻿, a linearly independent subset SSS﻿ is always smaller than/equal to a spanning subset TTT﻿ in size
    • proof: since TTT﻿ spans VVV﻿, we can write the elements of SSS﻿ as linear combinations of those of TTT﻿, and in matrix form:

      if m>nm> nm>n﻿, we can reduce AAA﻿ to its RRE form with a row of zeros, hence SSS﻿ not linearly independent, contradiction

  • all finite bases of a vector space have the same size; this size is defined to be the dimension of the vector space
    • proof: bases AAA﻿ is linearly independent and BBB﻿ spanning, so ∣A∣≤∣B∣|A|\le|B|∣A∣≤∣B∣﻿, and vice versa
  • the row/column ranks are defined to be the dimension of the row/column spaces
    • the row and column ranks of a matrix AAA﻿ equal, and the value is simply called rank(A)\text{rank}(A)rank(A)﻿

      proof: the EROs/ECOs do not affect the row/column spaces, hence RRE(A)\text{RRE}(A)RRE(A)﻿ and AAA﻿ have the same row/column ranks respectively

      RRE(A)\text{RRE}(A)RRE(A)﻿ has a block of identity matrix, with extra zero rows or columns, hence its row rank and column rank equal

      hence those of AAA﻿ equal too

  • in a finite vector space VVV﻿, basis SSS﻿ is a largest linearly independent subset, and a smallest spanning subset
    • proof (largest independent subset): if for contradiction SSS﻿ does not span VVV﻿, there is a vector v∈V,v∉⟨S⟩v\in V,v\notin \langle S\ranglev∈V,v∈/⟨S⟩﻿, then S∪{v}S\cup \{v\}S∪{v}﻿ is also independent, contradictory to the choice of SSS﻿
    • proof (smallest spanning subset): if for contradiction SSS﻿ not independent, removing the extra dependent vectors does not change the span (hence another spanning subset, but smaller), contradictory to the choice of SSS﻿
  • subspaces have smaller dimensions than the original vector space; equality only when the two are the same
    • proof: a subspace U≤VU\le VU≤V﻿ has its basis BU⊆VB_U\subseteq VBU​⊆V﻿, so it, being independent, is no larger than the basis of VVV﻿ (the largest linearly independent subset), hence dim⁡U≤dim⁡V\dim U\le\dim VdimU≤dimV﻿
    • when dim⁡U=dim⁡V\dim U=\dim VdimU=dimV﻿, BUB_UBU​﻿ is the largest independent subset by definition of dim⁡V\dim VdimV﻿, hence it is also a basis of VVV﻿, and U,VU,VU,V﻿ are the same, both being the span of BUB_UBU​﻿

The dimension formula, direct sums

  • in a finite vector space, any linearly independent subset can be extended to be a basis
    • proof: given linearly independent subset S⊆VS\subseteq VS⊆V﻿, if ⟨S⟩≠V\langle S\rangle \ne V⟨S⟩=V﻿, there is v1∉⟨S⟩v_1\notin\langle S\ranglev1​∈/⟨S⟩﻿, hence S∪{v}S\cup\{v\}S∪{v}﻿ is also a linearly independent subset.

      The process can be repeated to get larger independent subsets, but eventually terminates since the size of independent subsets is capped by dim⁡V\dim VdimV﻿ (dimension of the basis, a spanning set)

  • the Dimension formula: with finite vector spaces U,WU,WU,W﻿,
    • proof: with bases BUWB_{UW}BUW​﻿ of U∩WU\cap WU∩W﻿, extend it to be a basis of UUU﻿ and of WWW﻿ respectively, then their union is a basis of U+WU+WU+W﻿ since it is spanning and independent

      if ∑aiui+∑bjwj=0\sum a_iu_i+\sum b_jw_j=0∑ai​ui​+∑bj​wj​=0﻿, then U∩W∋∑aiui=−∑bjwj=0VU\cap W\ni\sum a_iu_i=-\sum b_jw_j=0_VU∩W∋∑ai​ui​=−∑bj​wj​=0V​﻿,

Linear Maps

  • a map is linear if it commutes with scalar multiplication and vector addition
  • composition, sum, and scalar multiples of linear maps are still linear
  • a linear transformation is bijective   ⟺  \iff⟺﻿it has a linear inverse   ⟺  \iff⟺﻿its domain and image has the same dimension
    • with basis {vi}\{v_i\}{vi​}﻿ being a basis of the domain, then {T(vi)}\{T(v_i)\}{T(vi​)}﻿ is a basis of the image, hence the two have the same dimension

Inner Product Spaces

Bilinear forms

  • bilinear forms on a vector space VVV﻿ is a binary operation that is linear on both variables
    • ordinary dot products are bilinear forms; in general, any B(x,y)=xAyTB(x,y)=xAy^TB(x,y)=xAyT﻿ is a bilinear form of coordinate vectors, where AAA﻿ is a matrix
  • a Gram matrix GGG﻿ of a bilinear form BBB﻿ w.r.t. a set of vectors {vi}\{v_i\}{vi​}﻿ is the matrix:
    • if the set of vectors is a basis, then B(X,Y)=xGyTB(X,Y)=xGy^TB(X,Y)=xGyT﻿, where x,yx,yx,y﻿ are the coordinate vectors of X,YX,YX,Y﻿ w.r.t. the same basis
    • thus matrices describes all bilinear forms in general

inner products

  • inner products are symmetric, positive-definite bilinear forms, written as ⟨−,−⟩\langle-,-\rangle⟨−,−⟩﻿
    • symmetric: ∀x,y∈V:⟨x,y⟩=⟨y,x⟩\forall x,y\in V:\lang x,y\rang=\lang y,x\rang∀x,y∈V:⟨x,y⟩=⟨y,x⟩﻿
    • positive-definite: ∀x∈V:⟨x,x⟩≥0\forall x\in V:\lang x,x\rang\ge 0∀x∈V:⟨x,x⟩≥0﻿, and [⟨x,x⟩=0]  ⟺  [x=0][\lang x,x\rang=0]\iff [x=0][⟨x,x⟩=0]⟺[x=0]﻿
  • inner product spaces are vector spaces equipped with an inner product
    • Rn\mathbb R^nRn﻿ with the ordinary dot product is known as Euclidean space
  • the length/norm of a vector vvv﻿ is ∥v∥=⟨v,v⟩\|v\|=\sqrt{\langle v,v\rangle}∥v∥=⟨v,v⟩​﻿, and distance dist(v,w)=∥v−w∥\text{dist}(v,w)=\|v-w\|dist(v,w)=∥v−w∥﻿
    • the length satisfies the triangle inequality: ∥v+w∥≤∥v∥+∥w∥\|v+w\|\le\|v\|+\|w\|∥v+w∥≤∥v∥+∥w∥﻿
  • the angle between vectors in an i.p.s. is defined as θ=arccos⁡⟨x,y⟩∥x∥∥y∥\theta = \arccos\frac{\langle x,y\rangle}{\|x\|\|y\|}θ=arccos∥x∥∥y∥⟨x,y⟩​﻿

Orthogonal maps

  • a map α\alphaα﻿ is orthogonal in a inner product space if ⟨α(v1),α(v2)⟩=⟨v1,v2⟩\langle\alpha (v_1),\alpha (v_2)\rangle=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle⟨α(v1​),α(v2​)⟩=⟨v1​,v2​⟩﻿
  • orthogonal maps have an orthogonal Gram matrix under the standard basis in Rcoln\mathbb{R}^n_{\text{col}}Rcoln​﻿
  • orthogonal maps are isometries, as they preserve inner products (which define distances)

  • complex inner product spaces need sesquilinear forms, i.e. linear in the first variable, and conjugate-symmetric: ⟨v1,v2⟩=⟨v2,v1⟩‾\langle v_1,v_2\rangle=\overline{\langle v_2,v_1\rangle}⟨v1​,v2​⟩=⟨v2​,v1​⟩​﻿
  • complex inner product spaces (aka Hermitian spaces) are vector spaces with a positive-definite, sesquilinear forms (aka Hermitian forms)
