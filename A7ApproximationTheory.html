Polynomial Interpolation

  • Given a function fff, we might try to approximate it with another (simpler) function ggg that agrees with it at a number of points: g:g(xi)=f(xi)  for i=0,…,ng:g(x_{i})=f(x_{i})\,\,\text{for }i=0,\dots,ng:g(xi​)=f(xi​)for i=0,…,nthen ggg is the intepolation function of fff at the (distinct) points x1,…,xnx_{1},\dots,x_{n}x1​,…,xn​.
  • If fff is difficult to work with, interpolating it with a polynomial g∈Πng \in \Pi_{n}g∈Πn​ gives a “similar” function that is easier to manipulate–in doing so, we hope that ggg and fff behave similarly.

Least-Squares Approximations

  • Given a function fff, we might want to approximate it with a polynomial p∈Πnp \in \Pi_{n}p∈Πn​. An alternative to interpolation is to explicitly find a polynomial that minimizes the “distance” between it and fff.

Numerical Integration

  • Numerical integration, aka quadrature, computes the integral ∫abf dx\int _{a}^{b}f \, dx∫ab​fdx for some integrable function fff, when a closed formula is not easily available.
  • All the techniques in the course are all (indirectly) derived from the integral of some approximant: fundefinedapproximatepundefinedintegrate∫abp dx≈pray∫abf dxf \xrightarrow{\text{approximate}}p \xrightarrow{\text{integrate}}\int _{a}^{b}p\, dx \overset{\text{pray}}{\approx} \int _{a}^{b} f \, dx fapproximate​pintegrate​∫ab​pdx≈pray∫ab​fdxwhere ppp is some approximant that is easy to integrate exactly.
  • Equivalently, their formulae will all be a weighted sum of data points: ∫abw(x)f(x) dx≈∑i=0kwif(xi)\int _{a}^{b}w(x)f(x) \, dx \approx \sum_{i=0}^{k}w_{i}f(x_{i})∫ab​w(x)f(x)dx≈i=0∑k​wi​f(xi​)where www is the weight function of the inner product (e.g. the Legendre weight w≡1w \equiv 1w≡1), and wiw_{i}wi​ are the quadrature weights.
  • The keys of this kind of quadrature are (1) the type of approximant used, and (2) the choice of data points. For the few methods mentioned in the course:
    • The trapezium rule integrates a piecewise linear function connecting the data points (xi,f(xi))(x_{i},f(x_{i}))(xi​,f(xi​)).
    • Simpson’s rule integrates piecewise quadratics fitted in intervals containing three data points.
    • Newton-Cotes quadrature integrates the Lagrange interpolation through equally spaced data points. Runge’s phenomenon makes the estimate terrible for certain functions.

Overview of Gauss Quadrature

  • Newton-Cotes shows that the Lagrange polynomial through equally spaced data points does not work. Maybe better chosen points will improve the result.
  • As an integral of the approximant, Gauss quadrature integrates the Lagrange interpolation pnp_{n}pn​ fitted through the roots of the orthogonal polynomial with degree (n+1)(n+1)(n+1): ∫abw(x)f(x) dx≈∫abw(x)pn(x) dx\int_{a}^{b} w(x)f(x) \, dx \approx\int_{a}^{b} w(x) p_{n}(x) \, dx ∫ab​w(x)f(x)dx≈∫ab​w(x)pn​(x)dx
