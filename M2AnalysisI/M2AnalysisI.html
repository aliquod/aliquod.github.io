Axioms and Fields

Arithmetic axioms of reals

  • Existence of addition, multiplication, add/multiplicative identities (0,1), inverses for each element (except multiplicative inverse for 0),
  • associativity and commutativity for add/multiplication; ⋅\cdot⋅﻿ distributes over +++﻿
  • 0≠10\ne 10=1﻿ (so that R≠{0} \mathbb{R} \ne \{0\}R={0}﻿ in total collapse )
  • the remaining properties of arithmetic can be deduced from those axioms

Fields

  • A set (with a ⋅\cdot⋅﻿ and +++﻿ defined) that satisfies the axioms is a field
  • e.g. R,Q,C\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{C}R,Q,C﻿ are fields, but Z\mathbb{Z}Z﻿ is not (no multiplicative inverses for most elements)

Bounds

  • supremum and infimum of a set are the smallest upper bound and largest lower bound
  • the completeness axiom of reals: for every bounded, non-empty real set, there is a supremum (and infimum)
    • R\mathbb{R}R﻿ is complete, but Q\mathbb{Q}Q﻿ is not.
  • if the supremum/infimum is a member of the set, then it is the maximum/minimum
  • the approximation property: given any ϵ>0,\epsilon>0,ϵ>0,﻿ there is x∈S:sup⁡S−x<ϵx\in S:\sup S-x<\epsilonx∈S:supS−x<ϵ﻿

  • density of rationals and irrationals: given a,b∈Ra,b\in\mathbb{R}a,b∈R﻿, there is x∈Q,y∈R\Q:x,y∈(a,b)x\in\mathbb{Q},y\in\mathbb{R}\backslash\mathbb{Q}: x,y\in(a,b)x∈Q,y∈R\Q:x,y∈(a,b)﻿

Countability

Definition

  • finite: bijection with {1,2,…,n}\{1,2,\dots,n\}{1,2,…,n}﻿ for some n∈Nn\in \mathbb Nn∈N﻿
  • countably infinite: bijection with N\mathbb NN﻿
  • countable: injection onto N\mathbb NN﻿
    • hence either countably infinite, or finite
  • uncountable: not countable

Deducing countability

  • unions and cartesian products of countable sets are countable
  • R\mathbb{R}R﻿ and the irrationals are not countable; Cantor’s diagonal argument

Monotone sequences

  • monotone sequences have all their terms either non-decreasing or non-increasing, i.e. for monotone decreasing sequences: ∀n,an+1≤an\forall n, a_{n+1}\le a_n∀n,an+1​≤an​﻿
  • the Monotone Sequence Theorem: a monotone, bounded sequence converges to its sup⁡\supsup﻿/inf⁡\infinf﻿
    • i.e. increasing sequence bounded above converges to its sup⁡\supsup﻿
    • proof (for increasing sequences):

      find S=sup⁡{an:n≥1}S= \sup\{a_n:n\ge1\}S=sup{an​:n≥1}﻿, which exists by completeness (non-empty, bounded set)

      then using the approximation property gives ∀ϵ:∣aN−S∣<ϵ\forall \epsilon:|a_N-S|<\epsilon∀ϵ:∣aN​−S∣<ϵ﻿ for some NNN﻿,

    • a monotone sequence not bounded goes to infinity

Subsequences

  • subsequences are sequences with elements from some original sequence, and in the right order
  • if (an)(a_n)(an​)﻿ converges, so does all its subsequences, and to the same limit
    • hence if two subsequence converge to different limits, the original sequence diverges
  • Scenic Viewpoints Theorem: every real sequence has a monotone subsequence
    • points ak:∀n>k,an<aka_k: \forall n>k, a_n<a_kak​:∀n>k,an​<ak​﻿ are called scenic viewpoints (or peaks)
    • proof:

      let VVV﻿ be the set of the peaks

      if VVV﻿ is infinite, then the members form a monotone decreasing subsequence

      if VVV﻿ is finite, then for each point beyond the last peak, there is a point higher than it; thus inductively take such points gives a monotone increasing subsequence (i.e. given ama_mam​﻿, find an>am,n>ma_n>a_m,n>man​>am​,n>m﻿ and add to the sequence and repeat)

  • Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem: a bounded real sequence has a convergent subsequence
    • proof: immediate from Monotone Sequence Theorem and Scenic Viewpoints Theorem

Rearranging Series

  • series that converge absolutely are robust, i.e. can be arranged without changing its limit
  • rearranging series that do not converge absolutely might change its limit, or make it diverge
    • work with partial sums that can be rearranged safely to simplify, then take the limit
